Growth Lab Working Paper No. 264 [ GL ]

A two-mineral economy at a crossroads

Mongolia rode the 2003–2013 commodity supercycle into the upper-middle income bracket. The boom built schools, paved Ulaanbaatar, and lifted eight hundred thousand people out of poverty. It did not build an economy that can grow without it.

In 2024, eighty-six percent of Mongolia’s merchandise exports were minerals; copper concentrate and coal alone accounted for seventy-two percent, up from forty-nine percent in 2010. Real GDP growth has swung between minus 1.3 percent and plus 17.3 percent over the past decade, tracking commodity prices rather than productive diversification. Mongolia ranks eighty-seventh of one hundred and thirty-three countries on the Economic Complexity Index, essentially unchanged over a decade. The boom enriched the country without broadening its productive base.

The export basket has concentrated rather than diversified. Copper concentrate and coal accounted for forty-nine percent of merchandise exports in 2010; by 2024 the figure had climbed to seventy-two percent. Outside mining, the productive base has hardly broadened, and the few tradable non-mineral sectors that grew during the boom — cashmere, light manufacturing for the Russian market — have stagnated or contracted since.

Macroeconomic volatility is a structural feature of the current configuration, not a bug. Real GDP growth has swung between minus 1.3 percent and plus 17.3 percent over the last decade, and each cycle erodes private investment in non-mining tradables. Mongolia has saved far less of its resource rents than the configuration of the economy requires: of an estimated USD 11.4 billion in cumulative rents since 2010, only USD 1.6 billion has been transferred to the Future Heritage Fund.

The country’s capability gaps lie in services and light manufacturing rather than in deeper extraction. Economic Complexity Index analysis shows Mongolia’s feasible diversification frontier passes through agribusiness, food processing, tradable business services, and the lighter end of the manufacturing spectrum. The window is closing as global coal demand peaks: the IEA’s central scenario sees seaborne thermal-coal demand peaking before 2030. Mongolia must build its non-mineral export engine before the current one stalls.

Foundations

The system stands on two type families and a small set of ink colors. Everything else — every heading, label, and caption — is built by varying the size, weight, and case of these two families against the ink palette. Resist adding a third family or new colors; reach for the existing tokens first.

Serif — long-form, display

Source Serif 4
Italic and optical sizes:
Aa Aa · Aa Aa Aa
Weights used: 300, 400, 500, 600

Use the serif for the cover title, all section & subsection headings, the lead paragraph, the colophon, and footnote markers. Always honor the optical-size axis: the cover title sits at opsz 60, section heads at opsz 36, body and captions at opsz 14–18.

Sans — UI, labels, body

Inter
Weights used: 400, 500, 600, 700
Aa Aa Aa Aa

Use Inter for body copy, table headers and cells, TOC sub-entries, page chrome (running head, folio, footnotes), eyebrows, and any small uppercase label. Always pair tabular numerics (font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums) on numeric columns.

Ink & accent palette

Text is layered: black for emphasis (--ink), near-black for body (--ink-2), and warm grey for secondary text, captions, page chrome, and hairlines (--ink-3). The accent blue is reserved for eyebrows, brand marks, and the cover date.

  • --ink-2#2C2823Body copy default, table cells
  • --ink-3#4F4A42Captions, eyebrow on cover, page chrome, hairline rules, sub-sub TOC
  • --accent#1A5A8ECover date, eyebrows

Decision rules

The system is small on purpose. Before introducing a new style, work through this list.

  1. Serif or sans? Serif for the cover, all headings, the lead, and the colophon. Sans for everything else.
  2. Body size is 12 px. Do not push body text below 12 px. Footnotes (9 px) are the only exception, and they live next to the folio.
  3. No trailing periods on headings. Section headings, subsection headings, and TOC entries end without a period.
  4. No all-caps body or headings. Uppercase is reserved for eyebrows, running heads, and similar 9–11 px chrome.
  5. Color is for emphasis, not decoration. Default ink layers: --ink (titles, strong), --ink-2 (body), --ink-3 (captions, chrome, hairlines). Reach for --accent only for eyebrows and the cover date.
  6. Tabular numerals in tables. Always set font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums on numeric cells, page numbers, and TOC page numbers.
  7. One lead per section. A p.lead never appears twice on the same page.
  8. Cover meta is two lines. Series name in ink-3 weight 600; date in accent weight 700. Never combine them on one line.
  9. No monospace. Numerals use Inter with tabular figures. Avoid mono fonts in body and tables.
