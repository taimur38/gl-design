Foundations — type & ink

Every chart is built from two type families and a small set of ink colors. Resist adding a third family or introducing new neutrals; reach for the existing tokens first.

Serif — chart titles & sources

Source Serif 4
Weights used: 400, 500; italic for sources

Used for the chart title (14 px, weight 500) and the chart source line (12 px italic). Nothing else inside the figure block uses serif.

Sans — everything else

Inter
Weights used: 400, 500, 600

Subtitles, axis labels, axis ticks, series labels, annotations, and legends. Always set font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums on numeric ticks and any numeric tabular data so digits align.

Ink palette

  • --ink#1A1714Chart title, axis line, strong emphasis
  • --ink-2#2C2823Axis labels, tick labels, annotations
  • --ink-3#4F4A42Subtitles, secondary captions
  • --gridline#ECE9E2Horizontal/vertical gridlines inside the plot
  • --accent#1A5A8EFigure label, primary single-series default

Chart color palettes

Categorical — main palette

Seven categorical hues, each available in three tones: light, main, and dark. The main tone is the default fill for every mark — lines, bars, treemap. The dark tone is reserved for scatter-circle strokes and all text associated with the colors (direct labels, legend entries, callouts, annotations). The light tone is used for backgrounds, faded states, highlight bands, and the lightest steps of sequential ramps. Tokens follow the pattern --c-N-light / --c-N / --c-N-dark.

  • c-1#B5D5EA · #2F87C8 · #1A5A8EPrimary — single-series default
  • c-2#E89C9C · #CC4948 · #8A2C2BContrast / lead-finding red
  • c-3#92D6BF · #2AA584 · #1A6B53Third series
  • c-4#B5A0CC · #7554A3 · #4A3470Fourth series
  • c-5#F4BC8A · #EA822D · #A8580FFifth series
  • c-6#E6E2A8 · #CDC86B · #8A8638Sixth series
  • c-muted#CDD2D9 · #999FA8 · #5F6773De-emphasis — “everyone else”

Reading the tokens: --c-1-light (#B5D5EA) for a faint highlight wash; --c-1 (#2F87C8) for the line, bar, or treemap tile; --c-1-dark (#1A5A8E) only for the stroke of a scatter circle. The same pattern repeats for every hue.

Sequential — single-hue ramps

Used on choropleth maps and any ordered encoding from low to high. Darker = higher value. Pick the ramp whose hue best matches the variable’s meaning or the surrounding chart palette. The examples below show five steps, but the number of steps should be adjusted based on the intended data representation — three for a coarse classification, seven or more for a finer gradient.

Diverging palettes

Used where data has a meaningful midpoint or reference point (gains vs. losses, above vs. below average). The boundary between the two hues marks the midpoint. Choose the pair whose semantics fit the variable. The examples below show six steps, but the number of steps should be adjusted based on the intended data representation — fewer for a coarse signal of direction, more for a finer gradient.

Axes, ticks, gridlines

Axes carry the frame of reference; they should be present but quiet. Use a single 1-px line for each axis, short outward ticks, and pale gridlines only where they help the reader estimate values.

Marks — circles, lines, areas

Every category color comes as a fill / stroke pair. Scatter circles are the only mark that uses 0.8 fill opacity — every other filled mark (bars, areas, treemap tiles, choropleth polygons) renders at full opacity. The stroke is always a darker tone of the same hue at full opacity.

Scatter circle — the only mark with reduced fill opacity. Lets overlapping points darken visibly to signal density.

Scatter plot

Use a scatter plot when the story is the relationship between two continuous variables. Each point is a category-fill circle at 0.8 opacity with a darker stroke at the same 0.8 opacity. Overlap is desirable — it shows clustering.

Line chart

Use a line chart when the story is change over a continuous variable, usually time. Up to four series; beyond that, mute the supporting lines in --c-muted and let the focus series carry the finding.

Stacked chart

Stacked bars (or areas) show how a total composes. Use up to six categories, ordered from largest mean share at the bottom upward. Fills are the main palette color at full opacity, no stroke. A 1 px gap separates each segment from the one above — this gives the boundary between categories a clean edge and helps readers with low color discrimination tell adjacent segments apart.

Figure 3

Services took over the export basket.

Export composition by sector, 2010–2024, share of total exports.

Source: Growth Lab analysis of national statistics.

Two or three tone option

Whenever possible, try to limit the use of different colors and use the different tones of the same color instead. You can also do this for categorical data: when two or three categories share a common parent — goods vs. services, low/medium/high tier, primary/intermediate/final — encode them with a single hue at its light, main, and dark tones rather than reaching for three unrelated colors. The shared hue keeps the bar reading as one total; the lightness difference carries the split. The 1 px gap between segments still applies.

Figure 3b

Services overtook goods in the export basket.

Goods vs. services share of total exports, 2010–2024, percent.

Source: Growth Lab analysis of national statistics.

Two tones, c-1 main and c-1-light. The same pattern works with any other categorical hue — --c-2 with --c-2-light, --c-4 with --c-4-light, and so on. Reach for it when the chart sits inside a section where that hue already carries narrative weight.

Two or three tone option

When a third category fits within the same parent concept, extend the same idea to all three tones of the hue: --c-1-light, --c-1, and --c-1-dark. Stacked-area form is the natural reading: the layers flow continuously across time, and the lightness ramp itself carries the ordering — lightest for the lowest tier at the bottom, darkest for the highest at the top. This is the one place outside a scatter plot where the dark tone is allowed.

Figure 3c

High-complexity exports grew share over the decade.

Composition of merchandise exports by complexity tier, 2010–2024, percent.

Source: Growth Lab analysis of UN Comtrade. Complexity tiers derived from PCI percentile.

Three tones, c-1-light / c-1 / c-1-dark. The dark tone is allowed here because all three bands belong to the same parent variable; the reader sees one ordered hue, not a scatter-circle stroke. The same approach scales to any hue (--c-2-light / --c-2 / --c-2-dark, etc.). Area bands sit edge-to-edge with no stroke and no gap — the lightness step is what separates them.

Tree map

Use a tree map when the story is the composition of a whole across many categories with very uneven shares. Each tile uses category fill at 0.8 opacity and the matching darker stroke at full opacity along its edge.

Radar chart

Use a radar chart to profile a single entity across four to eight dimensions on a shared normalized scale (0–1, 0–100, or any common index). The polygon’s shape carries the story; its area is a quick read of overall standing. The fill is --c-1 (#2F87C8) at fill-opacity: 0.25 so the gridlines and axis labels remain readable through it; the outline uses the same hue at full opacity, 2 px.

A balanced complexity profile, weakest on growth volatility.

Six-dimension capability index, normalized 0–1, latest year.

Source: Growth Lab analysis. Dimensions normalized to the cross-country max in each axis.

Geomap — sequential

Use a sequential choropleth when the encoded value runs from low to high without a natural midpoint — population, GDP, complexity. Five steps on a single blue hue; darker means higher. Polygons render at full opacity; a 0.5-px ink-3 stroke separates regions.

Figure 6

Complexity is concentrated in the central provinces.

Economic Complexity Index by province, 2022.

Source: Growth Lab analysis. Provinces stylized for illustration.

Geomap — diverging

Use a diverging choropleth when the value has a meaningful midpoint — change vs. baseline, gain vs. loss, above vs. below the national average. Red for the negative tail, blue for the positive tail, near-white at the midpoint. Polygons full opacity; ink-3 stroke at 0.5 px.

Implementing the pop-up effect

Color should only be used when it is necessary. When you have categorical data, you do not have to color every category differently. Always ask first whether the story can be told by a pop-up effect instead — paint the supporting data in --c-muted (#999FA8) and reserve a saturated hue (usually --c-1, sometimes --c-1 and --c-2 together) for the one or two series the reader actually needs to track. The muted layer carries the trend; the highlight carries the finding.

Decision rules

Before adding a new chart style, work through this list.

  1. Seven-color palette including a muted color. Colors are added in order — --c-1 first, then --c-2, --c-3, and so on. All six categorical colors should only be used if there is absolute necessity. If a visualization needs more than six, the representation mode should be re-thought.
  2. The darker tone is for strokes and all text associated with the visualization. Strokes around scatter circles use the dark version of the fill colour; every direct label, legend entry, callout, and annotation also uses the dark tone. The main tones do all the fill work — only stroke and text reach for the dark variant. This is required to clear WCAG AA contrast against the white background.
  3. Overlapping encodings get a 0.8 opacity. Whenever marks can sit on top of each other — scatter circles, area bands, overlaid polygons — apply fill-opacity: 0.8 and stroke-opacity: 0.8 together. The shared 0.8 lets overlapping marks darken visibly into the underlying layer, signalling density, while non-overlapping marks still read as the full main tone. Single-layer marks (bars, treemap tiles, choropleth polygons) stay at full opacity.
  4. Axis line and ticks are 1 px --axis (#2C2823). Ticks are 4 px long, outward, never inward.
  5. Gridlines are 1 px --gridline (#ECE9E2). Use them only where the reader needs to estimate a value off the axis; never both X and Y unless the chart is dense.
  6. Axis labels: Inter 12 px / 500 / --ink-2. Tick labels: Inter 12 px / 400 / --ink-2 with tabular-nums.
  7. Color encodes meaning, not decoration. If a color is not earning its keep — a category, a threshold, a highlight — remove it.
  8. Whenever possible, implement the pop-up effect. Mute the supporting data in --c-muted and reserve a saturated hue for the one or two series the reader actually needs to track. See Section 13 for worked examples.
  9. Sequential ramp for ordered values; diverging ramp only with a meaningful midpoint. Never use diverging for purely positive scales.
  10. Chart title ends with a period. It reads as a finding statement. Subtitle does not.
  11. No monospace. All numerals use Inter with font-variant-numeric: tabular-nums.
