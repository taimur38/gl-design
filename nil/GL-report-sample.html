A two-mineral economy at a crossroads

Mongolia rode the 2003–2013 commodity supercycle into the upper-middle income bracket. The boom built schools, paved Ulaanbaatar, and lifted eight hundred thousand people out of poverty. It did not build an economy that can grow without it.

In 2024, eighty-six percent of Mongolia’s merchandise exports were minerals; copper concentrate and coal alone accounted for seventy-two percent, up from forty-nine percent in 2010. Real GDP growth has swung between minus 1.3 percent and plus 17.3 percent over the past decade, tracking commodity prices rather than productive diversification. Mongolia ranks eighty-seventh of one hundred and thirty-three countries on the Economic Complexity Index, essentially unchanged over a decade. The boom enriched the country without broadening its productive base.

The export basket has concentrated rather than diversified. Copper concentrate and coal accounted for forty-nine percent of merchandise exports in 2010; by 2024 the figure had climbed to seventy-two percent. Outside mining, the productive base has hardly broadened, and the few tradable non-mineral sectors that grew during the boom — cashmere, light manufacturing for the Russian market — have stagnated or contracted since.

Macroeconomic volatility is a structural feature of the current configuration, not a bug. Real GDP growth has swung between minus 1.3 percent and plus 17.3 percent over the last decade, and each cycle erodes private investment in non-mining tradables. Mongolia has saved far less of its resource rents than the configuration of the economy requires: of an estimated USD 11.4 billion in cumulative rents since 2010, only USD 1.6 billion has been transferred to the Future Heritage Fund.

The country’s capability gaps lie in services and light manufacturing rather than in deeper extraction. Economic Complexity Index analysis shows Mongolia’s feasible diversification frontier passes through agribusiness, food processing, tradable business services, and the lighter end of the manufacturing spectrum. The window is closing as global coal demand peaks: the IEA’s central scenario sees seaborne thermal-coal demand peaking before 2030. Mongolia must build its non-mineral export engine before the current one stalls.

Four reform priorities

The reform agenda that follows in this paper, developed in detail in Section 5 and in the underlying thematic studies, is built around four mutually reinforcing priorities: stabilise the macroeconomic foundation, save the rents, build the missing capabilities, and deploy the people.

The first priority is a counter-cyclical fiscal anchor. The Fiscal Stability Law’s structural-balance rule should be replaced with a price-cycle–adjusted ceiling tied to a moving average of benchmark commodity prices. Surpluses generated above the ceiling should flow to the Future Heritage Fund automatically, on formula rather than ministerial discretion. The current configuration treats every boom as a surprise; a properly designed anchor treats booms as the norm and saves against them.

The second priority is the reform of Erdenes Mongol. The state mining holding currently combines commercial and political functions in ways that compromise both. A Norwegian-style commercial mandate, with arms-length governance, full IFRS reporting, and a board appointed against a transparent skills matrix rather than a political quota, would bring Mongolia’s sovereign mining footprint into line with the global standard for resource-rich middle-income countries.

The third priority is a capability-targeted foreign direct investment strategy. The complexity analysis identifies a finite set of feasible diversification opportunities: food processing, light manufacturing for the Northeast Asian market, and tradable business and IT services. None of these emerge spontaneously. A proactive Investment Promotion Agency, mandated to identify the missing capabilities behind each opportunity and to recruit the firms that carry them, is the institutional vehicle best suited to closing those gaps.

The fourth priority is the activation of the diaspora. Some one hundred and eighty thousand Mongolians work abroad in skill-intensive roles in South Korea, Germany, the United States, and Japan. The skills they have acquired are precisely the kind that the diversification frontier requires. Mapping the diaspora, building return-incentive programs around joint ventures with the firms that employ them, and formalising their role as commercial anchors in target export markets is the lowest-cost capability-acquisition strategy available.

None of the four priorities stands alone. Each presupposes the others; together they describe a coherent transition from an economy funded by rents to one that produces more of its own income. The remainder of this paper sets out the diagnostic evidence and the design of each priority in turn.

A boom built on two minerals

Two things explain almost everything about Mongolia’s economy since 2003: the price of copper, and the appetite of Chinese steel-makers for coking coal. When both rose, Mongolia ran one of the fastest growth rates on Earth. When either stalled, so did the country.

The 2003–2013 supercycle, anchored by the Oyu Tolgoi copper development and the Tavan Tolgoi coal field, was an extraordinary decade for Mongolia. Real GDP grew 9.0% per year on average. Foreign direct investment peaked above 50% of GDP in 2011 — a level few economies have ever recorded. Mongolia transitioned from low-income to upper-middle income status in less than ten years.1

What the boom did not produce was a more diverse productive base. The shape of the export basket on the eve of the boom looked very much like its shape today, only smaller. Cashmere, hides, and a thin layer of light manufacturing have effectively held flat in absolute terms while the mineral complex has tripled around them. The result is an economy whose nominal GDP looks transformed but whose productive structure — the mix of capabilities embedded in what Mongolia makes — has moved very little.

The Herfindahl-Hirschman story

The Herfindahl-Hirschman index (HHI) of export concentration tells the same story in a single number. An economy with a perfectly diversified basket scores near zero; one with a single export scores 10,000. Mongolia’s HHI rose from 1,840 in 2003 to 3,260 in 2024 — a level the World Bank classifies as “highly concentrated” and which puts Mongolia in the company of unitary-commodity economies like Botswana (diamonds) and Mauritania (iron ore).

The contrast with Chile — an economy often invoked as a peer for Mongolia — is instructive. Chile and Mongolia both had copper as their principal export in the 1990s. Chile’s HHI has fallen by 40% since; Mongolia’s has risen by 80%. The difference is not geology but policy.

The diversification gap

Concentration is not, on its own, the problem. Norway is concentrated in oil; Switzerland is concentrated in pharmaceuticals and watches. The problem is what concentration does to an economy that lacks the institutional buffers to manage it.

Mongolia’s GDP has tracked global commodity prices with almost mechanical fidelity for two decades. Boom and bust have replaced growth and stability as the operative pair. Each cycle redistributes capital toward mining and away from non-mining tradables, and each bust draws down fiscal buffers that should have been rebuilding for the next downturn.

The downstream effects show up not in the headline GDP number, which can be flattering during boom years, but in the underlying productive structure. Real wages in non-mining tradables have stagnated since 2014. Manufacturing employment has fallen as a share of the workforce every year since 2012. Foreign direct investment outside mining has averaged 0.4% of GDP in the past five years — an order of magnitude lower than peer economies in Southeast Asia.

Mongolia is poorer than its complexity predicts

The Atlas of Economic Complexity uses the diversity and ubiquity of a country’s exports to estimate the productive knowledge embedded in its economy — the Economic Complexity Index (ECI). Across the cross-section, ECI is a strong predictor of long-run income per capita, and the deviation from the trend line is itself a powerful predictor of future growth: countries that sit below the line tend to grow faster, as if catching up to their latent productive potential.

Mongolia sits above the line. Its GDP per capita is higher than its complexity would predict, because mineral-rents inflate its income beyond the productive base that supports it. The implication is uncomfortable: Mongolia is not a fast-converging economy in disguise. Without an active capability-building agenda, it is more likely to regress toward the level of income its productive structure can sustain.

The takeaway is not that mineral wealth is bad — it is that mineral wealth without capability investment leaves an economy in a structurally precarious position. The Sovereign Wealth Fund and the policy regime around it are how Mongolia translates today’s rents into tomorrow’s productive base. Both, at present, are underperforming.
